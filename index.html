Principles

Three things it refuses to compromise on.

No telemetry to negotiate, no settings to harden. The privacy is the architecture — and the prediction only gets quieter, never louder.

01 / 03

Private by construction

Every suggestion is generated on your Mac — by Apple's on-device model or a local one. There's no account, no sync, no server to opt out of, because there is no server. What you type is never transmitted, logged, or used to train anything. Zero bytes leave the machine
02 / 03

Every field, not some

Native apps through macOS Accessibility, plus Electron and web apps like Slack and Chrome through a keystroke engine. The same ghost text in Mail, Notes, a browser address bar, a search box. One behaviour, everywhere you place the cursor. System-wide, app-agnostic
03 / 03

It learns your voice

Few-shot learning from what you accept, an auto-built glossary of your own jargon, and an optional style document. Confident predictions read sharp; uncertain ones fade toward grey, so you always know how much to trust before you press Tab.

A small utility, built deep where it counts

01

Ghost text that never intrudes

The suggestion lives in a floating overlay that never touches your field — it can't pollute undo or fight your cursor. Only when you accept does a single character get typed.

02

On-device inference, your choice of engine

Apple's built-in Foundation Models, or a local model served by a bundled ollama on localhost — no install, no account, no server.

03

Word by word, or the whole line

Tab accepts the next word and you can chain it; one key takes the entire suggestion; Esc waves it off. Type straight through it and the ghost just keeps pace.

04

It learns the words you actually use

A personal glossary captures your company terms and acronyms, and Mind Reader quietly notices the names you type often and offers to remember them — spelled and capitalised the way you write them.

05

Per-app instructions

Different guidance for email, chat and notes, layered on top of your global style. Each app gets its own register.

06

Context-aware, on your terms

It reads your clipboard, the text after your cursor, and — only if you allow it — what's on screen via on-device OCR, so the continuation fits the moment.

07

Pixel-aligned overlay, multi-monitor honest

It matches the field's real font size and follows you correctly across displays, including an external monitor as the main one. No flicker, no drift.

08

Streaming suggestions

With a local Ollama model, words appear in the ghost as they're generated, so it feels instant instead of waiting for a whole sentence to land.

09

You set how much it writes

Suggestions come short (a nudge of a few words), medium, or long — most of a sentence. Short when you just want a hint; long when you'd rather it finish the thought.

10

A quiet record of what it saved you

A local dashboard tallies words completed, rough time saved and how often you accept — with a per-day chart and a daily streak. It lives in a SQLite file you can open in Finder or export to CSV. Nothing leaves the Mac.

First launch

Opening a self-signed app

Mind Reader is signed by its author, not the App Store — the Accessibility and key-tap APIs it needs can't run in the sandbox. The first time you open it, macOS shows a "could not verify" notice.

Click Done (not "Move to Trash"), then open System Settings → Privacy & Security, scroll down, and click Open Anyway. Once per Mac. Comfortable in Terminal? Skip it entirely:

xattr -dr com.apple.quarantine "/Applications/Mind Reader.app"

Up and running in a couple of minutes

Download & unzip

Grab the latest build and move Mind Reader.app into your Applications folder.

Open it the first time

Double-click. On the "could not verify" notice click Done, then System Settings → Privacy & Security → Open Anyway. macOS asks once; after that it just launches.

Grant the two permissions

In Privacy & Security, enable Accessibility and Input Monitoring (and, if you like, Screen Recording for screen context). Pick a model in Preferences and start typing.

Yes. Suggestions are generated on your Mac — either by Apple's built-in Foundation Models or a local Ollama model on localhost. Nothing you type is sent to any server. Mind Reader keeps a small local history (in SQLite) that it uses to learn your style — it stays on your Mac and is never uploaded.

Any standard text field — native apps like Notes, Mail and TextEdit through macOS Accessibility, plus Electron and web apps like Slack and Chrome via a keystroke fallback. Code editors (VS Code, Cursor) aren't a focus and are best left disabled — they already have their own completion. You can mute Mind Reader in any single app with one click — "Pause in …" in the menu bar, or the per-app blocklist in Preferences.

With one toggle. Google Docs draws your text onto a canvas and hides it from assistive apps by default, so Mind Reader can't see what you're typing. Turn on Tools → Accessibility → "Turn on screen reader support" (⌘⌥Z) and Docs starts exposing the text — then Mind Reader works there too. Other web editors like Gmail, Notion and Outlook on the web don't need this.

Mind Reader is self-signed rather than distributed through the App Store — the Accessibility and event-tap APIs it relies on can't run in the App Store sandbox. On first open, click Done on the warning, then System Settings → Privacy & Security → Open Anyway (once per Mac) — or clear the quarantine flag in Terminal. Because every update uses the same signing certificate, your permissions survive upgrades.
